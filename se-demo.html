How to run a demo that doesn't suck

Most demos fail because people jump straight into features. Here's the framework I use instead. Works for software, consulting, whatever you're selling.

1

Open by proving you listened

First 2 minutes. Don't touch the product yet.

Reference your previous conversation. Name their specific problems using words they used. Show you did your homework before showing a single screen.

Set expectations: how long this will take, what you'll cover, that they can interrupt whenever.

The point: You're not presenting. You're continuing a conversation about their problem.

2

Mirror their pain back

Make them feel understood before you solve anything.

List out what you think their problems are. Be specific. Include the impact of each problem if you know it.

Then ask: "Does this accurately capture what you're dealing with?"

If they correct you, that's gold. You learn something and they feel heard.

The point: Confirmation before solution. They need to nod yes to the problem before they'll care about the answer.

3

Show solutions mapped to their problems

The actual demo part. One problem at a time.

For each pain point you named in step 2, show exactly how it gets solved. Use real examples if you can. Show the before/after.

Keep connecting back: "Remember how you said X was a problem? Watch what happens here..."

The point: Features are boring. Solutions to their specific problems are interesting.

4

Make the ROI concrete

Numbers they can actually use.

Don't be vague. "You'll save time" means nothing. "Based on similar companies, you're looking at 25% faster [X] which on your current volume means [specific number]" means something.

Show the math. Let them argue with the assumptions if they want—that means they're engaged.

The point: They need to justify this to someone. Give them the ammunition.

5

Close with a clear next step

Not "let me know what you think."

Summarize what you showed. Connect it back to their problems one more time.

Propose something specific: a pilot, a technical deep-dive, a timeline. Ask what would need to happen for them to feel confident moving forward.

If there are objections, this is when they come up. Good. Address them now, not over email later.

The point: End with momentum, not with "we'll be in touch."
